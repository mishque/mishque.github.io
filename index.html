Rain Rain Go Away

Installation, Storytelling, Digital Print

Story

Compiling the 6 pieces of paper that contain my story was one of the toughest thing I have ever done. Summarizing the text was the first hurdle, but the toughest part for me was to think of illustrations or symbols to create patterns for the boats. To think visually, I had to imagine the events reoccurring and then translate those memories into lines and shapes.

Process

I created various prototypes of the boats before I landed at the final pattern, I wanted the text to be inside the boats as the make the audience unravel the story as they go along. I wanted the whole reading experience to be private. The act of folding hundreds of boats was theraputic, it was a nostalgic feeling. It made me access two very contrasting memories.

Thoughts

At the opening, I was happy to see that people were grabbing boats. The responses that I got from some was that they didn't want to unfold them at the exhibition and read them at home, some also mentioned that they were initially very gentle with the carefully folded boats but then as they got into the story they started unfolding them more furiously. I am happy that I did not crowd the story with any other elements like the water cloth and the bags I was planning on adding to the piece. I was also really happy with the shadows that they boats created on the walls.

FurniTurn

This is an ongoing experiment with furniture and fabric. Given that it is senior year, it feels like it is the end of an era and I am compelled to leave my mark in some way. This furniTurn series allowed me to leave my mark on furniture, transforming it with color and patterns.

  • Spray Legs

    Antique Table

    A graduating student passed down this table to me, she had originally gotten it from her aunt in Florida. This was my first project within this series of furniture and my first experiment with masking tape and spray paint.

  • Elephants in the Room

    2 Wooden Chairs

    I "borrowed" these chairs from an area near campus. This was my first attempt at upholstering a piece of furniture. My color and pattern choices were influenced by typical West Indian furniture.

  • Chili Chair

    Swirly Chair

    The entire time I have had my own studio on campus, I have been trying to think of a way to leave a mark, the trace of my exisitence in the studio. This chair was the perfect opportunity to do exactly that, the original leather was tearing off and the chili patterned cloth I had complimented it's beige metal frame.

  • T Time

    Table

    I found this table outside a dumpster, the size of it reminded me of tables that are found along side street eateries in India. I decided to cover it with a chai screen prints. As prints are paper, I am able to see the traces of my activities (indentation of items, spills, etc) on the table.

Dulari

Print, Web Design, Social Outreach

I have been working with this artist for the part year or so. I met her while traveling to Bihar, India with my mother and grandparents. From Dulari's shy exterior, I could never have guessed the kind of artist she was, it was only she took me around her small village to the various houses she creates her work in that I realized I was being shown around by a legend. Her style of art is called Madhubani, an artform that has been prevalent in the middle region of India in Bihar for generations. Dulari started painting when she was a little girl and usually painted after her job as a house servant. All the figures she paints are always facing side ways except for gods, she says she only liked drawing sides of faces because the nose was too difficult. I walked with her to 3 houses, hers, her mentors and one of her friends, the most acclaimed artist in town. All three houses were decked up in paint and Madhubani style imagery. I was blown away by the consistency of the line work and simple beauty. She told me she works with bright colors as those are the only paints (children's hobby paints) available in the area. Throughout my time with her, I realized that this unbelievable individual produced art with whatever she could find and that she definitely deserved more. My mother and I ended up purchasing some of her work, being a widowed woman in India, she was looked down on in society and relied on her work as her main source of income. A source the family could not have forseen to be so fruitful. Sadly she did not have an idea of the worth of her work, that her ornate paintings and sarees were worth much much more than her quoting price. As she told me the prices I argued with her to charge more and create her sense of worth as an artist, I was trying to get her to understand how rare her work was. After we purchased a few of her works, I wanted some of her information but she said she only had one copy of her resume and no business cards. This gave me an idea, I could help her get herself out there by providing her with her own business cards. It would also give me the opportunity to design a brand for someone I care so deeply about and who also serves as a great inspiration to my work. I have also been working on an interactive storybook, which will make her book accessible to more people.

FArchive

Since I moved to the US for college in 2011, I only get the opportunity to go back to my hometown, Ahmedabad for two or three days every year. In the time that I have spent abroad, I find myself getting more and more nostalgic of my life back home. However, my memories of home have begun to feel distant and lost in the past. To strengthen these memories, I began investigating my ancestral past. In my last visit in 2013, while rummaging through the closets in my grandmother’s house I came across a small plastic packet filled with square photographs. These were pictures of children, people on holiday and going about their daily activities. I quickly realised the young children in the pictures were my aunts and uncles and the photographer was my late grandfather, Iqbal Laliwala. This book is an archive of those images, categorized by subject matter. This book immortalizes these images for me and has brought me closer to where I originated from.
